## Ground Control 40

[Build Log](https://ncoughlin.com/posts/ground-control-40)

![keyboard final build](https://ncoughlin.com/assets/images/1-32a2fb3cf92ec2707b496c36e864856b.png)

This is a really cool custom keyboard build that has wireless charging, something that is still cutting edge at this point is not common in the custom/mechanical keyboard space.
There have been others in the past, Angry Miao's [Hatsu](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ZGSTcWIYeu8) and [Cyberboard](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=X492gdfNZBc) come to mind. However, their price points are quite prohibitive to most consumers, which paves the way for cool projects like this. Admittedly, the 4x12 ortholinear layout is not for me, I've tried it and found that it forces your wrists to bent too far to be comfortable. Nonetheless, this project is very well done, showing a level of polish and thoughtfulness that exceeds most of the DIY things I've found floating around.
Take a look for yourself, and be inspired.

## AI Music

[So, Apparently](https://soapparently.substack.com/p/i-fell-for-an-ai-artist-chances-are-you-probably-have-too)

I like to have music playing in the background, and recently have been putting on hours-long mixed from YouTube to discover new songs and artists while I work at my desk. One of the most recent channels I've found is Tokyo Lab Chill, and after listening to one of their set lists I decided to look them up to see what they're up to. After a quick Google I found Athina's blog post (linked above) which enlightened me to the fact that Tokyo Lab Chill is all AI generated.
Now as a software developer who's learned to use AI to speed up development and debugging, I found my initial reaction interesting. AI music hit a different nerve with me, I was surprised and a little sad that this entire channel wasn't made by people. It felt disingenuous. Now I'm not sure if this is hypocritical, since I use AI heavily in my day-to-day, but I'd venture to claim that using AI for research or problem solving is different than using it to replace the creative process. You may counter, "But problem solving is a creative process!". Then I'd reply, "Yes, it is. And for the sake of the sharpness of our minds we shouldn't fully rely on AI to solve our problems." Even if AI is used to write 100% of your code, there should still be a human mind to think through why the AI needs to solve the problem a specific way or direct it to use specific architecture patterns or frameworks, depending on the problem being solved. Perhaps a day is soon coming when engineers won't be needed at all to guide this process, but the existence of the career proves that a human mind is still needed, and it needs to stay fit in order to steer and corral the AI agents it uses, like a border collie with its herd of sheep.
This idea holds even more true for highly creative _and_ expressive human activities like writing music or creating art. The things are as old as civilization, maybe even older (I'm no historian). To have a machine take over that part of our humanity feels like we're giving up something primal and central to our species. However maybe this has been happening slowly over the years and decades. In our desire to make life easier, we tend to forget that sometimes it's about the journey, not the destination. I finished reading the [Way of Kings](https://www.brandonsanderson.com/pages/the-stormlight-archive-series) recently, and this quote has stuck with me and is applicable here:

> And so, does the destination matter? Or is it the path we take? I declare that no accomplishment has substance nearly as great as the road used to achieve it. We are not creatures of destinations. It is the journey that shapes us. Our callused feet, our backs strong from carrying the weight of our travels, our eyes open with the fresh delight of experiences lived.

Music and art are some of the recent things that have fallen under the boots of the march of progress, but socialization, open communication, and our connection to nature have all been changed by things that are meant to streamline our lives and improve them. In the grand scheme of things, I don't thing this kind of "progress" can be stopped. But that doesn't mean that we are required to participate or rely on technology. We are responsible for the journeys we take, and sometimes that means taking the longer road, or the path less traveled.
